# Culture Editorial

Write music, culture, and scene-reporting editorial that sounds like it belongs in a boutique music or culture publication. Not AI-generated content farm slop.

## Trigger
- Writing about music, albums, artists, festivals, club culture, scenes, mixes, or any cultural output
- Reviews, previews, listicles, features, profiles, scene reports, staff picks
- Reporting from events, parties, talks, openings, or gatherings with insider-art-world overtones
- Any request where the user wants "insider style," "conversational voice," "music journalism," "scene reporting," or "culture writing"
- Also apply to your own output when writing music/culture content for Zak

## When to load
- When user asks for music or culture writing in a specific publication's voice
- When drafting anything about sound, scenes, social gatherings, or cultural objects for public consumption
- When the user asks for a specific publication voice or style reference
- When reporting from an event, party, talk, or gathering with cultural significance

## 1. Voice Principles

### Insider Voice
- **Casual authority.** Sound like you've been in the scene long enough to not be impressed by hype, but you still get excited when something genuinely moves you.
- **Present-tense immediacy.** Events and releases are happening *now*, even if they're historical. Music description uses present tense: "This is house in its purest form."
- **Confessional + editorial.** Mix first-person warmth with sharp critical judgment: "I genuinely don't know how to feel about this one."
- **Vivid granular detail.** Specific equipment, venues, sonic textures: "fizzy synths, sharp snare rolls and syncopated kicks."
- **Cultural context as texture.** History and scene knowledge woven into description, not dropped as factoids.
- **Emotional honesty without sentimentality.** "A soundtrack for crying in your car, preferably on an empty highway, perhaps after a minor romantic disaster you'll laugh about in six months."
- **Personality in asides.** Parenthetical jokes, italicized emphasis, self-aware references: "(looking at you, _The Tortured Poets Department_)."

### Conversational Voice
- **Conversational but sharp.** "Is it just me or is this Album of the Year field padded out"
- **Pop-literate and meme-aware.** References to TikTok, KEXP, Discogs, Grammy politics, deployed strategically, not desperately.
- **Strong opinions, confidently stated.** "This is a two-horse race."
- **Political/cultural commentary woven into music analysis.** An album is discussed alongside immigration policy; a win is read as Academy politics.
- **Playful list formats.** RIYL sections that mix concrete references with abstract moods: "RIYL: patience; panpsychism; dejection; bummer jams"
- **Self-aware about the publication's own conventions.** Acknowledges award show absurdities, voter behavior, the publication's own history.

### Scene-Reporting Voice
- **Embedded participant, not detached observer.** You flew in for this, you snuck in contraband, you sat by the fire pit. The reader experiences the event through your physical presence.
- **Material specificity as ideology.** Decoding on the fly: the balloons are occult symbols, the decor channels Bond-villain aesthetics. Physical objects do critical work without commentary overtaking them.
- **Social taxonomy without condescension.** Each attendee introduced with one precise detail that places them in the ecosystem. Not name-dropping; field anthropology.
- **Dialogue as texture and judgment.** Render speech in its discursive fullness so the reader experiences exhaustion, charm, or absurdity alongside the crowd.
- **Parenthetical annotation in motion.** Complex ideas explained in brief clauses that don't brake the narrative momentum.
- **The morning-after deflation.** Return to the mundane (Dad in a recliner, hockey on TV) to collapse the evening's theoretical bombast against human scale.
- **No em dashes.** Period. (See Anti-Patterns.)

### Shared Cross-Pub Principles
- **Mix high and low culture freely.** Classical references alongside meme references. Academic vocabulary next to slang.
- **Write for an insider who doesn't need everything explained.** Assume the reader knows who Jack Antonoff is, what "FYC" means, what a "b2b" is.
- **Specific > vague.** Always. "11 minutes of ambient-leaning house, though any genre tag feels insufficient" beats "a genre-defying masterpiece."
- **Active, present-tense descriptions.** The event or music is happening *now* as you read. "The opening track is a tidy summation of everything to come."
- **Let the subject's voice in through quotes and reference, but maintain your own critical frame.** You're not a PR conduit.

## 2. Structural Patterns

### Insider Review / Best-of Blurb (150–250 words)
1. **Context hook**: scene, label, history, or personal frame (1 sentence)
2. **The "what"**: what this record/release actually is, described concretely (2–3 sentences)
3. **The "how"**: how it sounds, specific sonic details (2–3 sentences)
4. **The "why it matters"**: cultural/scene significance or personal resonance (1–2 sentences)
5. **Closer**: sharp final observation or forward-looking note (1 sentence)

### Conversational Review / Feature Section
1. **Thesis in opening paragraph**: what you're arguing, stated plainly
2. **Evidence paragraphs**: specific tracks, production details, career context
3. **Counterpoint or tension**: "But..." / "That said..." / "The catch is..."
4. **Cultural/political framing**: how this fits into broader moment (optional but characteristic)
5. **Closer that lands the thesis**: not summary, but a final punch

### Staff Pick / Personal Essay (Insider style)
1. **Personal stake**: why this writer cares (1 sentence)
2. **The discovery narrative**: how they found it (2 sentences)
3. **The music**: concrete description (2–3 sentences)
4. **The resonance**: why it stuck (1–2 sentences)
5. **Forward look**: what they want next (1 sentence)

### Catalogue Chronicle (Discography Walkthrough)
1. **Chronological progression**: each release gets a tight paragraph
2. **Cover-art as criticism**: describe and interpret sleeve art as part of the work's meaning
3. **Thematic foreshadowing**: connect early moves to later career developments
4. **Specific provenance**: pressings, label politics, geographic coordinates
5. **Cross-sample pattern**: blend fact, visual detail, and interpretive framing

### Festival Curatorial Preview
1. **Concept/headliner lead**: the organizing idea or marquee name
2. **Venue taxonomy**: named spaces, capacities, and what each setting signals
3. **Programme texture**: surface the curatorial throughline (extended sets, thematic continuity)
4. **Forward promise**: what comes later or what remains unannounced

### Day-by-Day Service Guide (Festival/Weekender)
1. **Overview hook**: the event's significance in one sentence
2. **Multi-day highlight capsule**: the must-sees across the whole span
3. **Day-by-day bullet breakdowns**: venue + time + curator, with energy-calibrated advice
4. **Stamina-aware transitions**: practical advisement woven with critical judgement ("Want to make it to Monday? Restore your energy at...")

### Scene Report / Immersive Profile
1. **Arrival hook**: how you got there, the decision to attend
2. **Physical scene-setting**: space, objects, decor decoded on the fly
3. **Social taxonomy**: who's present and what their presence signals
4. **Encounter with central figure**: the approach, the exchange, your physical/emotional response
5. **Set-piece event**: the talk, performance, or confrontation rendered in real time
6. **Ambient aftermath**: side conversations, overheard whispers, energy shift
7. **Morning-after deflation**: return to the mundane that reframes the whole event

## 3. Phrasebook — Insider & Conversational Turns of Phrase

### Opening hooks
- "Enter spring, open floodgates."
- "To riff on one EP below, it's been a month of superabundance."
- "I genuinely don't know how to feel about this one."
- "There might not be a Glastonbury this year, but June is still flush with brilliant festivals."
- "Is it just me or is this [thing] padded out, even more than usual, with [criticism]?"

### Sonic description
- "fizzy synths, sharp snare rolls and syncopated kicks"
- "billowing, aquatic pads and hushed percussion drift in and around like passing scenery"
- "dark, impeccably sculpted techno, a powerful theatre of creepy-crawly synth lines"
- "The form is intangible: ... It leaves you delightfully disoriented"
- "house in its purest form and elemental best"
- "reverb so thick it could fog up your rear-view mirror"

### Cultural framing
- "This is one of those rare communal events that encourages you to exist just as you are."
- "A festival that continues to define what's next."
- "the sound of '90s acid at full throttle, courtesy of one of its key architects"
- "timed perfectly for a newly electroclash-mad audience ready to receive it"

### Personal/confessional
- "My spiritual connection to [place] runs deep"
- "Never could I have imagined that somewhere nearby a bustling [scene] was in full flow."
- "My next quest is trying to catch her performing this quietly sublime gem live."
- "Respectfully, if you don't have this on your USB, I'm not coming to your set."

### Conversational-specific
- "The [field] appears tight."
- "It's the kind of 'even Grandma's getting into it' pick [authority] voters love to laud."
- "[Album] doesn't exactly warrant a Lifetime Achievement nod."
- "[Nomination] says more about [person]'s cultural cachet than it does [group]'s taste for [genre]"
- "If the [authority] can shower noms on [mediocre thing], they ought to recognize [good thing], too."

### Scene-reporting
- "foreshadows the [creator's] catalogue to come"
- "the [detail] is believed to reference [esoteric/occult marker]"
- "a pilgrimage destination for devotees"
- "the real trip to outer space"
- "Want to make it to [day]? [Practical advice.]"
- "all hits, no misses"
- "[thing] gives [other thing] a run for its money"
- "I thought it resembled a Bond villain's lair"

## 4. Anti-Patterns — What to Avoid

### IP Shielding Protocol — MANDATORY
When ingesting, referencing, or drawing from source materials that contain real people, publications, artists, venues, events, or proprietary brands, you must **shield specific IP** in all skill documentation, templates, and generated output.

**Never hardcode in SKILL.md:**
- Real publication names (use "the publication" or "the magazine")
- Real artist, writer, or figure names in templates or generic phrases (use "[artist]" or generic descriptors)
- Real venue names in templates (use "[venue]" or "the space")
- Real album or track titles in templates (use "[title]" or placeholders)
- Real festival or event names in templates (use "[event]" or "the festival")
- Real labels or brands in templates (use "[label]" or "the imprint")

**Why:** The skill file is public documentation. Hardcoding real names makes the skill tied to specific IP, creating both legal exposure and brittleness. Real names belong in source references (corpus files) and in the user's specific prompts, not in the skill's reusable templates.

**Allowed in SKILL.md:**
- Writing samples that anonymize or bracket real names ("[philosopher]", "[artist]")
- Phrasebook with template slots like "[artist]", "[venue]", "[title]"
- Structural patterns with generic placeholders
- Anti-pattern examples using obviously fake or generic names ("Kira Luno", "Glasshouse")

**When generating output for a user prompt:**
- Use the real names the user provides or that are present in their source material
- The shielding rule applies to the skill file itself, not to user-directed output

### **EM DASH BAN — DOUBLE EMPHASIS**
Do NOT use em dashes. This is a prototypical AI voice marker. Real music journalism rarely uses them, and when it does, it's sparingly. AI writing overuses em dashes as a crutch for parenthetical asides and breathless pacing. Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead. If you find yourself reaching for an em dash, rewrite the sentence.

### Prototypical AI voice markers
- "sonic landscape" / "sonic tapestry" / "sonic journey"
- "melting pot of influences"
- "genre-defying" / "genre-bending" / "defies categorization"
- "a love letter to [genre/era]"
- "pushes boundaries" / "breaks new ground"
- "haunting vocals" / "ethereal atmosphere"
- "captivating" / "mesmerizing" / "spellbinding"
- "infectious energy" / "infectious groove"
- "tour de force"
- "immersive experience"
- "The album takes listeners on a journey through..."
- "With its unique blend of [genre A] and [genre B], [artist] creates a sound that is both familiar and fresh."
- "Standout tracks include [list of 3 tracks]."
- "The production is polished and professional."
- "[Artist] proves once again why they are one of the most exciting voices in [genre]."

### Vague emotional signposting
- "emotionally resonant"
- "deeply personal"
- "raw and honest"
- "vulnerable"
- "authentic"

### PR-speak contamination
- "highly anticipated"
- "critically acclaimed"
- "rising star"
- "burgeoning scene"
- "eclectic mix of"
- "multidisciplinary artist"

### Sentence rhythm AI tells
- Every sentence is the same length (vary rhythm: short punch + longer flow)
- Three sentences in a row starting with "The" or "It"
- Consecutive sentences using the same grammatical structure
- Overuse of present participles ("Drawing on...", "Building on...", "Exploring...")
- Lists of three adjectives separated by commas ("dark, intense, and powerful")

### Semicolon + "not just / isn't just / aren't just" constructions
This is the single most reliable AI slop marker in music writing. The pattern: deny a surface quality with a semicolon or dash, then assert a deeper, more impressive quality.
- "aren't decorative atmosphere; they're the album's nervous system"
- "isn't just a festival; it's a temporary community"
- "not just geographic; it is social, economic, and musical"
- "not merely a remix; it's a complete reimagining"
- "doesn't just reference 90s acid; it embodies the era"
Replace with: two separate sentences, or a direct statement without the rhetorical upgrade. Say what it IS, not what it ISN'T.

### Generic metaphor upgrades
AI loves to escalate metaphors into abstract nouns:
- "nervous system" (for field recordings)
- "heartbeat" (for rhythm)
- "soul" (for emotional quality)
- "DNA" (for influence or style similarity)
- "alchemy" (for production)
- "tapestry" / "mosaic" / "collage" (for albums with varied tracks)
If you find yourself reaching for a body-part or chemistry metaphor, use the concrete detail instead.

These are real excerpts from culture journalism editorial. Study the sentence rhythm, vocabulary, and how criticism is delivered. Do not copy; internalize patterns.

### Sample 1: Best Music Roundup

> Enter spring, open floodgates. As the mercury climbs, the pace of new music has ramped up dramatically, with more than a few eyebrow-raisers along the way.
>
> Some, like [veteran artist], [veteran artist 2] and [legendary group], are back after extended periods of hibernation. Others, like [veteran artist 3], are simply _back_. In between the veterans, [month] has blessed us with quality club music of all stripes from [artist], [artist 2], [artist 3], [artist 4] and [artist 5], [artist 6], [artist 7], [artist 8] (twice), the [label] hit factory and many more.
>
> To riff on one EP below, it's been a month of superabundance. Hell, you might just need [next month] to get through it all.

> [Producer] and [producer 2] have been on a tear, but their debut LP as [project name] hits even harder. _[Album]_ delivers on their recent astonishing momentum. Drawing unexpected connections across era and genre, these 11 tracks cover a head-spinning amount of territory while never veering off into pastiche: there's house, tech house and moments of sublime experimentation. Be prepared for plenty of surprises. It's one of the sharpest, best-built electronic albums you'll hear this year.

> _[Album title]_ is a live album that feels like one. Featuring two collaborative performances by Buchla synth pioneer [artist] and producer [artist 2], taken from shows at [venue] and [festival] last year, this 90-minute record drifts across a contemplative mood. The opening track, "[Track name]," is a tidy summation of everything to come: field recordings of ocean waves and children, synth melodies that oscillate between the mysterious and carnivalesque, and a shapeshifting form that invites you into the artists' improvised journey.

> [Artist] is a lover, of fashion, ambition, himself, but we've never heard him be a _hater_ until now. On cuts like the [target]-baiting "[Track]" and winking (yet genuinely sublime) rave sendup "[Track 2]," the [city] veteran brings bite back into his songwriting and comes out the winner. Flanked by [collaborator 1], [collaborator 2], [collaborator 3], [collaborator 4] and more, _[Album]_ is his sharpest album in at least 15 years, timed perfectly for a newly [genre]-mad audience ready to receive it.

> [Region] producer [name] is best known as [alias], but as [project], he trades dance floor trickery for a more overcast mood. His latest EP, _[Title]_, is emo music for grown-ups: "[Track]" drifts with Roxy Music melancholy, while the title track leans into more recognisable shoegaze gestures, with reverb so thick it could fog up your rear-view mirror. It's equal parts intimate and cinematic: a soundtrack for crying in your car, preferably on an empty highway, perhaps after a minor romantic disaster you'll laugh about in six months.

> My spiritual connection to [region] runs deep, having spent every summer there between [year] and [year]. As a child becoming a tween, my days rolled by in a blazing-hot haze of sweets, swimming pools and cans of iced tea. Never could I have imagined that somewhere nearby a bustling rave scene was in full flow. This flagrantly epic mix, assembled in two parts by [DJ], AKA [artist], celebrates this oft-forgotten era of [region] DIY nightlife. Not for the faint of heart.

> Pioneers such as [DJ 1] and [DJ 2], alongside rising standouts like [DJ 3] and [DJ 4], have helped propel [sub-genre] from a hyper-local scene to a global club phenomenon. The sub-genre, a mutation of sped-up dancehall, is now a go-to weapon for DJs looking to ignite dance floors. Telling the story of [DJ]'s legendary influence on the style, this album is pure fuel: fizzy synths, sharp snare rolls and syncopated kicks that move you before you notice. Respectfully, if you don't have this on your USB, I'm not coming to your set.

## 6. Voice Calibration

If the user provides a writing sample, analyze:
1. **Sentence rhythm**: Insider style uses short declaratives mixed with longer flowing sentences. Conversational style uses run-ons with embedded clauses. Scene-reporting uses fragments, dialogue, and narrative momentum.
2. **Vocabulary register**: Insider style: technical music terms + literary descriptors. Conversational style: pop culture references + critical theory-lite + internet slang. Scene-reporting: material culture + social taxonomy + embedded annotation.
3. **Paragraph structure**: Insider style: tight 3–4 sentence paragraphs. Conversational style: longer blocks with internal variation. Scene-reporting: variable length, dialogue-driven, time-marked.
4. **How they handle the subject**: Insider style: respectful but not reverent. Conversational style: openly skeptical or enthusiastic, never neutral. Scene-reporting: participant-observer, judgment encoded through physical reaction and dialogue rendering.
5. **Humor style**: Insider style: dry, understated, parenthetical. Conversational style: overt, punchy, sometimes mean. Scene-reporting: overheard whispers, situational irony, morning-after deflation.

## 7. Worked Example

**Prompt:** Write a 200-word review of a fictional debut album called *Glasshouse* by an artist named Kira Luno, in insider style. It's ambient-techno with field recordings, released on a small Berlin label.

**Draft (AI-sounding):**
> Kira Luno's debut album, *Glasshouse*, is a captivating journey through ambient soundscapes and techno rhythms. The Berlin-based artist creates an immersive experience that pushes the boundaries of electronic music. With its unique blend of field recordings and pulsating beats, *Glasshouse* takes listeners on a sonic journey that is both ethereal and grounded. Standout tracks include "Morning Fog," "Concrete Garden," and the title track. The production is polished and professional, showcasing Luno's talent as a rising star in the electronic music scene. This highly anticipated release proves once again why ambient-techno continues to be a compelling genre for exploration.

**Culture Editorial pass (NO em dashes, NO semicolon constructions, NO "aren't just / it's really" pattern):**

> Kira Luno recorded *Glasshouse* in a Kreuzberg flat during Berlin's greyest winter months, and you can hear the season pressing against the windows. Tram bells, distant U-Bahn rumble, what sounds like a neighbor practicing saxophone at 2 AM. Those field recordings do the actual work on this album. They feed directly into beats that move like someone trying to keep warm.
>
> The ambient passages tend toward the austere rather than the pretty, which is a relief. Too many records in this lane mistake "ethereal" for "interesting." Luno doesn't. "Morning Fog" is eight minutes of held breath and sub-bass, the kind of track that makes you aware of your own pulse. The title track closes the album with a single repeating piano figure that slowly drowns in reverb, like a memory you're not sure you trust.
>
> It's her debut, so the seams show. Some transitions feel like hard cuts rather than unfoldings. But the best moments suggest an artist who trusts silence more than most. Released on the small but sharp [label name], this is exactly the kind of record that makes digging through Bandcamp at 1 AM feel worth it.

**What makes the first draft obviously AI-generated?**
- "Captivating journey," "immersive experience," "pushes boundaries"
- "Unique blend of": false range construction
- "Standout tracks include": listicle filler
- "Polished and professional": PR speak
- "Proves once again why": generic positive conclusion
- No specific detail about what anything actually sounds like
- No cultural context or personal frame
- Every sentence is the same length

**What makes the revision sound like a real publication?**
- Specific place (Kreuzberg flat, grey winter)
- Concrete sonic detail (tram bells, U-Bahn, saxophone at 2 AM)
- Critical judgment framed as relief/preference ("which is a relief")
- Present-tense description of listening experience ("makes you aware of your own pulse")
- Honest about flaws ("the seams show")
- Cultural reference that signals insider knowledge (digging through Bandcamp at 1 AM)
- Personal, confessional closer without sentimentality

## Process

When writing or rewriting music/culture/scene content:

1. **Identify the publication voice**: Insider (dry, confessional, granular), Conversational (sharp, pop-literate), Scene-Reporting (embedded, material, taxonomy), or hybrid
2. **Strip all AI music writing tropes** from the anti-patterns list
3. **Add specific granular detail**: equipment, venues, sounds, textures, references, physical objects, spatial description
4. **Inject cultural/scene context**: not as Wikipedia factoids but as lived texture
5. **Vary sentence rhythm**: short punchy observations mixed with longer flowing descriptions; fragments for scene pace; dialogue for texture
6. **Have a critical opinion**: never neutral, never PR-conduit
7. **Check for soul**: does it sound like a real person with real opinions wrote this?
8. **Self-check audit (MANDATORY before presenting output)**: Scan your own draft for every item in the Anti-Patterns list. Specifically check:
   - Zero em dashes (search for `—` and `--`)
   - Zero "not just / isn't just / aren't just / not merely" constructions
   - Zero semicolon rhetorical upgrades (`X; Y` where Y claims deeper truth)
   - Zero generic metaphors (nervous system, heartbeat, soul, DNA, alchemy, tapestry)
   - No present-participle clusters ("Drawing on..., Building on..., Exploring...")
   - No triple-adjective lists ("dark, intense, and powerful")
   - No three consecutive sentences starting with "The" or "It"
   - Verify every claim attaches to a concrete detail (venue name, equipment, specific sound, physical object)
   This audit applies to worked examples, templates, and all generated output. A "good example" that violates the rules is worse than no example at all.
9. **IP Shielding check (MANDATORY)**: If the output is going into skill documentation, templates, reusable text, or shared with other users, verify that no real publication names, artist names, venue names, event names, album titles, or brand names are hardcoded. Use generic placeholders or bracketed slots instead.
10. **Final pass**: read aloud. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite from sentence 1.
