## RA: Best Music 2026: April (ra.co/features/4490)

> Enter spring, open floodgates. As the mercury climbs, the pace of new music has ramped up dramatically, with more than a few eyebrow-raisers along the way.
>
> Some, like Larry Heard, Untold and Boards of Canada, are back after extended periods of hibernation. Others, like Tiga, are simply _back_. In between the veterans, April has blessed us with quality club music of all stripes from Dresden, SnPLO, ZULI, Chris Stussy, upsammy and Valentina Magaletti, Loukeman, Ophelié, Priori (twice), the Djax-Up-Beats hit factory and many more.
>
> To riff on one EP below, it's been a month of superabundance. Hell, you might just need May to get through it all. – _Gabriel Szatan, Editor & Head of Editorial_

> Priori and Patrick Holland have been on a tear, but their debut LP as Jump Source hits even harder. _Fold_ delivers on their recent astonishing momentum. Drawing unexpected connections across era and genre, these 11 tracks cover a head-spinning amount of territory while never veering off into pastiche: there's house, tech house and moments of sublime experimentation. Be prepared for plenty of surprises—it's one of the sharpest, best-built electronic albums you'll hear this year.

> _Concrète Waves_ is a live album that feels like one. Featuring two collaborative performances by Buchla synth pioneer Suzanne Ciani and producer Actress—taken from shows at the Barbican Centre and Sónar music festival last year—this 90-minute record drifts across a contemplative mood. The opening track, "Concrète Waves London A1," is a tidy summation of everything to come: field recordings of ocean waves and children, synth melodies that oscillate between the mysterious and carnivalesque, and a shapeshifting form that invites you into the artists' improvised journey.

> Founded in 1989 by Saskia Slegers, AKA Miss Djax, Eindhoven label Djax-Up-Beats operated as a vital transatlantic conduit, connecting Chicago and Detroit with a rapidly expanding rave infrastructure in Europe. Curated by Rush Hour co-founder Christiaan Macdonald, this compilation spotlights standout records from the label's golden era. And as the title suggests, this opening instalment is an ode to the Roland TB-303's outsized impact on the formation of early dance music. This is the sound of '90s acid at full throttle, courtesy of one of its key architects.

> Tiga is a lover—of fashion, ambition, [himself]—but we've never heard him be a _hater_ until now. On cuts like the Musk-baiting "Sexless Pornographic Losers" and winking (yet genuinely sublime) rave sendup "I Am Your Detroit Sunrise," the Montréal veteran brings bite back into his songwriting and comes out the winner. Flanked by Priori, Fcukers, Gesloten Cirkel, Boys Noize and more, _HOTLIFE_ is his sharpest album in at least 15 years, timed perfectly for a newly electroclash-mad audience ready to receive it.

> Carla dal Forno's fourth album is her catchiest and most accessible yet. The Australian artist has an experimental streak that has always been tempered by an equally strong ear for a pop hook, and _Confessions_ sees her leaning towards the latter. There are still fuzzy, weird stretches here—"Under the Covers" sounds like a Bristol trip-hop band making a bubblegum record—but the album feels refreshingly honest with its clear-eyed brightness.

> As one of the world's biggest DJs, Chris Stussy could have probably phoned in his debut album, adding a few ambient interludes to whatever was on his hard drive with the world none the wiser. Instead, Stussy provides three discs that gather dusty B-sides, new collaborations and, of course, some of those bangers that have been sending the TikTok massive into meltdown for years ("Side to Side" is track two).

> Octo Octa loves house music. She really, really, _really_ loves house music. Her fourth album, _Sigils for Survival_, is an ode to all its varieties, from old-school hip house ("Survival Groove") to cerebral afterhours burners ("Hynpotic Circle") to acid rippers ("Just Listen") to jazzy Chicago funk ("Keep Pressing On"). This is house in its purest form and elemental best.

## RA: Top Ten Festivals 2026: June (ra.co/features/4484)

> There might not be a Glastonbury this year, but June is still flush with brilliant festivals. From Lighthouse's sun-soaked Adriatic escapism to Detroit's homegrown Tec-Troit and The White Hotel's anniversary celebration in Blackpool, here are ten festivals worth your attention next month. We're so ready.

> In the north of Croatia, near the Italian border, you'll find Lighthouse's flagship event. For five days each summer, the festival takes over an entire hotel complex, Lanterna Resort, on a small peninsula jutting out into the Adriatic Sea. With the comfort of a hotel room, excellent programming and no restrictions on bringing refreshments to site, Lighthouse fully lives up to its tagline: "Electronic music on vacation." The festivities go down along the coastline, tucked away in the woods and in a few shabby former club spaces—you can even check out 3D models of the various stages on the website.

> Conceived as a temporary community, Naturalis returns to Naples for three days of dance music designed for total immersion and respite from the hustle and bustle of the modern world. Taking place al fresco in the Ecoparco del Mediterraneo, the festival is built for comfort, with wellness activities and a selection of glamping options. The programming prioritises continuity over quick hits: extended DJ sets, indulgent pacing and seamless handovers across three stages—Fire, Earth and Water—so the weekend unfolds like one shared journey rather than a timetable of clashes.

> From the minds behind Unsound, Ephemera returns to Warsaw for its seventh and most expansive edition. The week-long festival splices music, performance, dance and visual art across contrasting venues where the room becomes part of the score. Collaboration and new pairings are central to the programme, with a range of one-off, specially commissioned performances. Drawing on Slavic ritual and forward-leaning experimentation, highlights range from Cabaret Voltaire to Arooj Aftab, Oli XL, Rosa Pistola, dj neurospicy and Young Boy Dancing Group.

> Drag wrestling, puppet theatre, comedy shows—this isn't your average festival. Combining avant-garde curation with nature, Dripping attracts a wide range of multidisciplinary creatives thanks to its wholesome mentality. The focus is also broader than just dancing or raging—workshops, on-site AA meetings, lake swims and free drug testing are among the many activities that also appeal to wellness-focused crowds. This is one of those rare communal events that encourages you to exist just as you are.

> In this day and age, it's no small feat for a festival to reach its 15th birthday. So this year's anniversary is a big one for Gottwood. Set on the grounds of Carreglwyd Estate, the beloved Welsh festival enjoys a uniquely beautiful setting and welcomes a loyal following of dance music heads, many of whom return year on year. New for 2026 is a 24-hour licence, which will be rolled out with careful programming across a selection of the stages. The lineup is something of a who's who of dance music right now, with stage partners including Half Baked, Hit & Run and Butter Side Up.

> At Solstice, midsummer begins with three days of dancing while the sun never sets. The festival takes place on an arctic fell in northern Finland, with breathtaking views out across pine forests and glassy lakes. From high-octane sets at the aptly named Peak Stage to ambient soundscapes in the Kelo Tent, Solstice's lineup has something for everyone, and every hour.

> The Barcelona institution returns with another deep dive into electronic music and digital culture. Since 1994, Sónar has balanced big-name draws with leftfield innovation, and the 2026 edition is no different, with a programme of forward-thinking club sounds, audiovisual premieres and experimental live shows. Musically, you can expect a typically sharp mix of performances that span bass music, hyperpop, techno and beyond, alongside Sónar+D's broad range of talks and showcases. A festival that continues to define what's next.

## RA: 26 Releases Our Staff Couldn't Stop Playing This Summer (ra.co/features/4463)

> Without question, everyone on the editorial and content teams at _Resident Advisor_ listens to tonnes of music. We write about it, DJ with it, some even produce it. We receive hundreds of promos a day, and it's our job to sort through the noise to give readers the most interesting records that, often to us, speak to a broader sonic culture. Inevitably things will fall through the cracks, or in the case of Addison Rae, there will be times when we just didn't know an album would be _that good_. And then there are the exciting tunes and mixes that are definitely not landing in our inboxes—the sneaky Bandcamp drops and chaotic SoundCloud streams that we have to dig for.

> This new feature series aims to cover our blindspots. For this first edition, we asked several staff members to select their favourite music—EPs, tracks, albums, mixes—from the past few months that we haven't yet covered as a publication. Some choices are earnest, others silly. Some are niche, others could hardly be more mainstream. These are our staff picks for summer 2025.

> New Jersey producer Daniel Fisher is best known as Physical Therapy, but as Car Culture, he trades dance floor trickery for a more overcast mood. His latest EP, _Nothingburger_, is emo music for grown-ups: "Coping Mechanism" drifts with Roxy Music melancholy, while the title track leans into more recognisable shoegaze gestures, with reverb so thick it could fog up your rear-view mirror. It's equal parts intimate and cinematic: a soundtrack for crying in your car, preferably on an empty highway, perhaps after a minor romantic disaster you'll laugh about in six months.

> If you know Dave Huismans from his dubstep project 2562, "#32 (Walrus)" might feel unfamiliar. The track opens with cicada-like hums and inhales and exhales with suspense, before blooming into 11 minutes of ambient-leaning house, though any genre tag feels insufficient. The form is intangible: billowing, aquatic pads and hushed percussion drift in and around like passing scenery. It leaves you delightfully disoriented, never quite sure where the next turn will lead.

> My spiritual connection to Andalucia runs deep, having spent every summer in the southern Spanish region between 1996 and 2004. As a child becoming a tween, my days rolled by in a blazing-hot haze of sweets, swimming pools and cans of iced tea. Never could I have imagined that somewhere nearby a bustling rave scene was in full flow. This flagrantly epic mix, assembled in two parts by Dj BonClient, AKA John Talabot, celebrates this oft-forgotten era of Spanish DIY nightlife. Not for the faint of heart.

> Luckily I receive promos for work, or else I'd have spent 2025 aching to get hold of New York musician james K's hyper-anticipated new album, _Friend_. The LP, which finally lands next month on AD 93, is a snowstorm of tender, hooky songwriting—the ideal soundtrack, as it turns out, for dawn wake-ups with my baby boy. In the last month, my favourite tune switched from "Blinkmoth (July Mix)" to "Doom Bikini." My next quest is trying to catch her performing this quietly sublime gem live.

> _Sirât_, the new film by Spanish filmmaker Óliver Laxe, opens with the mad jubilance of a desert rave that slowly unravels into tragedy. Without giving away too much of the plot, this movie is demented, and so is the music. Kangding Ray's immediately identifiable sonic signature—dark, impeccably sculpted techno—is the ideal accompaniment, a powerful theatre of creepy-crawly synth lines and ambient panoramas that perfectly stage _Sirât_'s sense of blistering anguish.

> It's hard to recall the last time I became as immediately obsessed with an artist as Blacklauren. Novel takes on peak-time techno come rarely in 2025, and even less so finding artists who reference Haitian vodou, Toni Braxton and Wata Igarashi in one release. One listen sparked a very thorough comb through of his discography, which is just as rich, raw and hypnotic as this EP. This is music that marries dynamic styles in one breathtaking, psychedelic swoop.

> Pioneers such as DJ Chuckie and DJ Moortje, alongside rising standouts like Styn and T.NO, have helped propel bubbling from a hyper-local scene to a global club phenomenon. The sub-genre, a mutation of sped-up dancehall, is now a go-to weapon for DJs looking to ignite dance floors. Telling the story of DJ Shaun-D's legendary influence on the style, this album is pure fuel: fizzy synths, sharp snare rolls and syncopated kicks that move you before you notice. Respectfully, if you don't have this on your USB, I'm not coming to your set.

## Pitchfork: Grammys 2026 Predictions

> Can Kendrick repeat? Will a K-pop star finally win Record of the Year? And who snags the inaugural Best Album Cover?

> The Grammy Awards return this Sunday, February 1, and will once again be hosted by Trevor Noah. It's the comedian's sixth—and, according to him, final—time leading the ceremony. Topping this year's list of nominees is Kendrick Lamar, with nine nods, including appearances in three categories he won in 2025: Record of the Year, Song of the Year, and Best Rap Performance. For the second-most nominees, it's a three-way tie between Lady Gaga, Jack Antonoff, and _Mayhem_ producer Cirkut, who have seven apiece.

> Whether any of these artists come home with a golden gramophone, though, remains to be seen. To find out, you'll have to watch this year's main ceremony, which will be broadcast live from Los Angeles' Crypto.com Arena, beginning at 8 p.m. EST on CBS and Paramount+. In the meantime, check out our predictions for who should and will win in key categories at the 68th Annual Grammy Awards.

> Come Grammys season, ubiquity usually trumps all else; the more beaten into the cultural short-term memory, the better. In those terms, the 2026 Record of the Year race appears tight. Promo for Chappell Roan's latest loosie "The Subway" has been plastered across its namesake public transit system all year. Lady Gaga's "Abracadabra" premiered during last year's Grammys. Bad Bunny, one of the biggest artists in the world right now, will bring "DtMF" to the Super Bowl halftime show on February 8. And not only has Doechii's single "Anxiety" been floating around for years, it's all but pre-existing IP, lightly reworking a Gotye song that won this category in 2013.

> But if there's one contender that was completely inescapable in 2026, it's "APT." Even with Academy darlings like Kendrick Lamar, SZA, and Billie Eilish in the mix, "APT." is the kind of "even Grandma's getting into it" pick Grammy voters love to laud. Bruno Mars is also a frequent flier in the Record of the Year category and Rosé's BLACKPINK beginnings fit the Grammys' recently-peaked interest in Korea's star-making machine. (If "APT." takes the statuette, she'll be the first K-pop artist to win the category.)

> But if it's top-to-bottom production and performance the Grammys are allegedly after, "Abracadabra" should win. The _MAYHEM_ single doesn't exactly warrant a Lifetime Achievement nod. But after a decade of the Academy pointedly rewarding Gaga's more traditionalist work, seeing her clinch her first Record of the Year for a song sharing more DNA with "Swine" than "Shallow" would be extremely satisfying.

> Is it just me or is this Album of the Year field padded out—even more than usual—with releases that have little to no chance of actually winning? _Man's Best Friend_ and _Swag_ are both impressive feats of pop craftsmanship, but feel minor compared to their older siblings; the inclusion of _Let God Sort Em Out_ says more about Pharrell's cultural cachet than it does Grammy voters' taste for coke rap; and if it seems like _GNX_ and _Chromakopia_ came out literal years ago, that's because they did.

> Leon Thomas' _Mutt_ is the only record on this list I haven't heard, which, based on the last five years of winners, means he has a solid 20% chance of taking home the trophy, but really, this is a two-horse race. In one lane, Lady Gaga, who successfully married her classic theatrics with a newfound air of hard-won grace in the rollout around _Mayhem_. In the other, Bad Bunny—never quite a Grammys darling, but a perennial presence in the awards' Música Urbana categories. _Debí Tirar Más Fotos_ is Benito's most musically audacious project and his most expressly political. As the Trump administration threatens to send ICE agents to his Super Bowl Halftime Show, the Recording Academy has the rare chance to throw its weight behind an artist at the exact moment when his work is as urgent, ambitious, and necessary as it's ever been.

> Surprised the Record of the Year and Song of the Year categories are roughly indistinguishable? Welcome to the Grammys. Although the former category technically focuses on production while the latter highlights songwriting, every year the winners for each end up looking like a spot-the-difference game. Much like my high-school's lacrosse coach, Grammy voters love to play favorites (how else does a buzzer-beater 2024 Billie Eilish single that received next to no FYC promotion end up in competition?)

> My money is on Kendrick Lamar and SZA's "Luther." It spent the most time atop the Hot 100, Jack Antonoff had a hand in it, and it's _far_ less incendiary than the track that rightfully cinched Lamar this category last year. But if we're heading for another Antonoff-happy Grammys, he should be recognized for Sabrina Carpenter's "Manchild," the best distillation of the newly-minted superstar's delightful shtick yet and certainly the tightest lyrical showing here. If the Grammys can shower noms on Antonoff's less-impressive layups (looking at you, _The Tortured Poets Department_), they ought to recognize his slam dunks, too.
